The Challenge

Background: The Faculty Toolbox was created as a resource for faculty to learn about the US Air Force Academy's Learning Management System (LMS). When I started my current role I took over the development of the Toolbox. There were several usability problems that were apparent from day one.

Goal: Redesign and rebrand the Faculty Toolbox to increase usability and traffic to the site.

My Role: Website Designer, User Researcher, Information Architect

The Design Process

User Research

I combined my experience as a faculty member and instructional designer along with informal interviews to create personas for the 5 different types of faculty I discovered during my research.

Common to all personas was a desire for information to be easy to find and the navigation to be “intuitive”. In this case, an “intuitive” navigation is one which maps a three-component mental model faculty have for building a course: course content and delivery, course administration, and assessment.

UX Methods

Along with user research, the main UX skills used in this project were:
  • Information Architecture: The original site navigation did not reflect the mental model faculty use for building courses, so I redesigned the site navigation and link names to reflect that model.
  • Sketching and Wireframing: Creating content templates required ideating, and then sketching and wireframing, solutions for presenting content to faculty.
  • UI Style Guide: The headers and fonts varied from page to page in the original Toolbox, so I created a style guide to standardize the look and feel of the site.

Design Decisions

Guided by the personas, I identified three problems with Toolbox as it waws created:

  1. Site navigation that was not “intuitive” because it did not reflect the mental model faculty have for course building.
  2. Content was not catered to the needs of the faculty at USAFA.
  3. No consistent use of fonts, headings, and colors to visually organize site content.

Site Navigation

The original site navigation did not reflect the mental model faculty use for building their courses.

For instance, "Inside Your Course" was too broad a label, and even when I opended it content wasn't organized according the mental model.

To remedy this, I sketched out prototypes for the top-level architecture and some secondary architecture.

Previous Next
After showing these ideas to my team and some faculty, it was clear that I was on the right track. So, after a little more tweaking, the Faculty Resources portion of the course menu was finished.

A big advantage of using this site navigation is that it also previews the LMS's capabilities when I open a page, such as "Building Your Course".

Catered Content

After restructuring the site navigation, I developed a three-stage approach to dealing with missing and misorganized content:

  1. Organize existing content in accordance with the new site map.
  2. Add priority content.
  3. Develop and implement a content roadmap, to include both the templates for content and the topics themselves.
The first two stages were, essentially, first aid. For instance, moving "Adding scrollbars to Grade Center on Macs" out of "Inside Your Course" and into "Additional Resources".

For the third stage, I started with content templates because it would allow me to:

  • Provide a consistent experience for faculty;
  • Ensure that the copy addressed the needs of the faculty through the design of the template;
  • Make it possible to outsource the "how to" guides by linking to the LMS help site; and
  • Speed up the copy writing process (which is helpful, since I am a one-man shop).
I quickly went from sketches of a few possibilities down to two different content templates, and developed two prototypes. [Carousel of shots here]

Style Guide

The style across the original Toolbox was inconsistent. [carousel] To provide a consistent user experience, I created a style guide for the Toolbox. To keep things simple, I stuck with the defaults in the LMS's text-editor for things such as font. I also added the following rules such as:

  • Black font for content.
  • Blue (#0000FF) font for in-page headers to help further organize content (such as when a list of quizzes begins).
  • Dark Cyan-Azure (#0099CC) for quizzes.
  • Use highlighting to emphasize important information such as warngings, known issues, and system downtime.
These rules provided a consistent user experience and helped visually chunk content on a page.

Results

  1. 46.3% increase in traffic since the redesign, and new users visiting the site every month.
  2. Over 50% of faculty have used the Toolbox and voluntarily adopted Blackboard in under two years ago and despite some very vocal critics.
  3. A consistent user experience across the Faculty Toolbox website.
  4. The implementation of design thinking in LMS-related training at the Academy.
